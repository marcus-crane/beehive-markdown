I te rangi nei I kî te Minita mô ngâ Take Mâori, a Parekura Horomia, kua whakaatu mai e ngâ hua rangahau, he whakanuinga ora tonu o te maha o te Mâori e taea te kôrero I te reo, me te whakaaturanga o te maha ake o ngâ mâtua Mâori kei te kôrero ki a râtou tamariki I roto I te reo, me te pai rawa atu o çnei âhuatanga.

I pânuitia e Horomia, te Tirohanga I te Oranga o te Reo Mâori 2006, ki Waiwhetu Marae, kei Awakairangi, I te râ nei.

Ko ngâ tîpakopako e tâpiri ana kei roto I te rangahau, ko te pikinga, mâ te iwa ôrau, o te maha on ngai Mâori e taea ana te kôrero I çtahi kupu ruarua, me ngâ kupu kîanga ki roto I te reo Mâori, mai I tçrâ tirohanga atu I te tau 2001.

“Ko te âhuatanga tino pai, ko te kitenga I te tôpûtanga o te 52 ôrau o te Mâori I uiuitia, I kî e kaha ana râtou ki te kôrero Mâori.”

“Anô, e whitu ôrau te pikinga o te Mâori kua kaha ki te kôrero tino pai, âhua pai rânei, I te reo. Ko tçnei e whakaata ana I te ia whânui o te tirohanga, me te whakaatu ko ngâ hua tohu tino nui, kei roto I ngâ taumata tohu teitei ake.”

Kua piki anô te kaha a te Mâori, ki te whakamârama (mâ te whakarongo), te pânui tuhituhi, me te tuhituhi – I çtahi kupu, kîanga ri roto I te reo mâ te waru, te 10, me te 11 ôrau mô ia tohu kaha.

“ Kua kaha anô te pikinga o te maha o ngâ taiohi Mâori, a kaha ana ki te kôrero I roto I te reo, me te maha o ngâ kaikôrero, e pupuri taumata tohu teitei ana, kua piki taurua I waenga I te ropû 15 – 35 te pakeketanga, tçnei âhuatanga whakamanawa,” e kî ana a Horomia.

E 13 ôrau te pikinga o te maha o râtou e 15 -24 tau te pakeke, ka taea te kôrero kia maha ake ki ngâ kupu iti, me ngâ kîanga, I roto I te reo, me te pikinga 16 ôrau o râtou mâ kei roto I te pakeketanga 25 – 34 tau.

“ He mea whakahirahira te whakakaha i te matatau I waenga I ngâ kapa taiohi kia tutuki ai te whakawhitiwhiti I te reo I waenga I ngâ reanga – pçrâ anô ki te kôrero I te reo I roto I ngâ kâinga. Koia nei te take me harikoa ki ngâ mâtua e kôrero ana I te reo ki roto I ngâ kâinga,” I kî a Horomia.

Kua piki anô mâ te 17 ôrau te maha o ngâ pakeke e kôrero Mâori ana ki roto I ngâ kâinga ki ngâ tamariki kôhungahunga, te pikinga 14 ôrau ki ngâ tamariki kei ngâ kura tuatahi, me te pikinga e 20 ôrau ki ngâ tamariki kura tuarua.

I tîmatatia e Te Puni Kôkiri te Tirohanga Rangahau o Aotearoa I te tau 2006 ki te whakaine I te ahunga whakamua o te reo, mai I te rangahau tuatahi e 5 tau ki muri.Tata ana ki te 4000 ngâ Mâori I uiuitia mô a râtou ake kaha ki te whakamahi I te reo.

Hei tâ Horomia, ko ngâ hua kei te tohu e anga whakamua ana ki te whakatutukitanga o ngâ whâinga a te Rautaki Reo Mâori.

“ Ka tautoko ngâ hua I te piriponotanga a te kâwanatanga, ki te whakahoutanga o te reo, te mea nui, ki te whakatû anô I te whakamahinga I te reo Mâori, ia râ, ia râ, I waenga whânau.”

“Engari he roa tonu te hîkoi ki te wâ e taea e tâtou te kî, kei te tino pakari 100 ôrau te reo Mâori. Kei roto anô I te rangahau e kî ana, he maha rawa atu ngâ tângata e taea te whakamahi I te reo, kahore râtou I te whakamahia. Me whai tonu tâtou ki te rapu huarahi ki te whakamahi I te reo ia râ ia râ, hei reira râ anô ka taea e tâtou te kî pono, kei te tino hauora pakari, ora hîranga rawa atu te reo.”

Ka takoto te târua o te Tirohanga atu ki te Oranga o te Reo Mâori ki runga I te ipurangii o Te Puni Kôkiri, atu I te 6 pm. www.tpk.govt.nz

Survey results showing a healthy increase in the number of Mâori who can speak te reo and revealing more Mâori parents are speaking te reo to their children hold significant promise, Mâori Affairs Minister Parekura Horomia said today.

Mr Horomia released the 2006 Survey on the Health of the Mâori Language at Waiwhetu Marae in Lower Hutt today.

Highlights of the survey include a nine per cent increase in the number of Mâori who can speak more than a few words or phrases in te reo Mâori since the last survery in 2001.

“A total of 52 per cent of Mâori surveyed said they could speak te reo to some degree which is great news.”

“There has also been a seven per cent increase in the number of Mâori who can speak the language either very well or fairly well. This reflects the general trend of the survey which reveals the greatest proficiency gains are among the higher proficiency levels.”

The ability among Mâori to understand (by listening), read and write – more than a few words or phrases in te reo has also increased by eight, 10 and 11 per cent respectively.

“There have been significant increases in the number of younger Mâori who can speak te reo and the number of speakers with high proficiency levels has more than doubled among the 15-35 age group, which is particularly heartening,” Mr Horomia said.

There has been a 13 per cent increase in the number of 15-24 years able to speak more than a few words and phrases in te reo and a 16 per cent increase among those aged between 25-34 years.

“Increasing competency among the younger age group is of critical importance in achieving intergenerational language transmission - as is the speaking of the language in the home. This why the increases in the number of parents speaking te reo at home are so pleasing,” Mr Horomia said.

There has been a 17 per cent increase in the number of adults speaking Mâori in their homes to pre-school children, a 14 per cent increase with primary school children and a 20 per cent increase with secondary school children.

The Research New Zealand Survey was commissioned in 2006 by Te Puni Kôkiri to measure the progress of the reo since the first survey was held 5 years ago. Nearly 4000 Mâori were interviewed about their Mâori language ability and use.

Mr Horomia said the results indicated significant progress towards the achievement of the goals of the Mâori Language Strategy.

“The results support the government’s commitment to the revitalisation of the language, particularly into re-establishing the normal, everyday use of te reo among whânau.”

“Still, we have some way to go before we can declare te reo Mâori 100% fighting fit. This survey tells us that a significant number of people who can use the language do not. We need to continue to find ways to make the use of Mâori a normal everyday activity and then we can safely say that the language is in robust and excellent health”.

A full copy of the Survey on the Health of the Mâori Language in 2006 will be available on the Te Puni Kôkiri website from 6pm. www.tpk.govt.nz
